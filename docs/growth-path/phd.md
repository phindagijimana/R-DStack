# PhD level

> You **define a research direction.**

At PhD level, you are the person who chooses the direction. You answer the question "what should we work on?" — for yourself, for collaborators, eventually for a group.

## Focus

- Original contribution.
- Strong theory and evidence.
- Field-level understanding.
- Rigorous validation.
- Reproducible systems.
- Publications and impact.

The shift from advanced is that you are no longer closing the most obvious gap. You are *choosing which gap to close*, defending the choice in front of experts, and producing knowledge and systems that meaningfully change what the field can do.

## What you actually do

- Identify and defend the most important open problem in your subarea.
- Design an original method or framework that addresses it.
- Build the systems needed to study, validate, and deploy it.
- Validate across datasets, sites, and conditions, with rigour that survives hostile review.
- Publish the work, release the code, and curate any new datasets.
- Mentor more junior researchers through their own projects.
- Begin to define the next generation of questions, not just answer the current one.

## Example output

> *A clinically validated biomedical AI framework for epilepsy biomarker discovery and decision support.*

Concretely:

- A framework that ties together multiple modalities (MRI, EEG, PET, clinical notes) into a coherent pipeline for biomarker discovery.
- An open multi-site dataset assembled for the work, including pre-surgical imaging and post-surgical outcome labels.
- A validated decision-support prototype used by clinicians at two or more institutions.
- Two or three high-quality publications: one method paper, one validation study, one framework/dataset paper.
- Open-source code, documented, with a user community.
- A thesis (or equivalent body of work) that defends the direction and synthesises the findings.
- Three follow-up directions clearly opened by this work, which other researchers can now pursue.

## What you are learning at this stage

- How to choose problems whose answer matters in five years, not five months.
- How to make trade-offs at the level of research strategy, not just method design.
- How to lead collaborations across institutions, disciplines, and seniority levels.
- How to defend originality, rigor, depth, contribution, and independence in front of expert critics.
- How to write for the field, not for the reviewer.
- How to recognise when a direction is mature enough to hand off and start a new one.

## Beyond the PhD

PhD-level work is not the end. It is the start of the work that comes after — postdoc, faculty, principal scientist, founder, technical lead. Each of those roles compounds the same qualities at larger scope: more people, more problems, longer time horizons, larger contributions.

The growth path does not stop at PhD. The roadmap just starts naming larger and larger groups of people you are setting direction for.
