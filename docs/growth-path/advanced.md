# Advanced R&D

> You **create a new system or method.**

At the advanced stage, the work shifts from improving what exists to building things that did not exist before. The artifacts have real users, the validation crosses datasets, and the direction is increasingly yours.

## Focus

- Identify field gaps.
- Design better workflows.
- Validate across datasets.
- Build usable software.
- Communicate contribution.

The shift from intermediate is that you stop being the implementer of someone else's plan and start being the planner.

## What you actually do

- Read the field deeply enough to articulate, in your own words, what is missing.
- Design a system or method that closes that gap, defending it against the closest existing alternatives.
- Build it as a system — not a script — with versioning, testing, documentation, and deployment.
- Validate it on more than one dataset, including at least one you and the system have never seen before.
- Communicate it through multiple channels: paper or preprint, code release, documentation, talks.
- Build and maintain a user base — even a small one — and respond to their feedback.

## Example output

> *Create a NeuroInsight-style platform that integrates MRI biomarkers, EEG findings, and clinical reports.*

Concretely:

- A platform that ingests structural MRI, functional MRI, scalp EEG, and clinical metadata for a patient.
- Runs a battery of analyses: hippocampal asymmetry, DWI tractography, EEG source localisation, functional connectivity.
- Synthesises the results into a clinician-readable report.
- Has been validated on at least two cohorts from different institutions.
- Is documented well enough for an external research group to install and use.
- Has at least one published paper describing the method and at least one preprint or paper describing validation.

That platform is a single artifact carrying the four marks of advanced R&D: new methods, real users, multi-site validation, and an open, documented contribution.

## What you are learning at this stage

- How to recognise a gap that the field has not yet named.
- How to choose where to spend complexity (some pieces of an advanced system must be simple; others must be sophisticated).
- How to handle the messy reality of multiple datasets — different conventions, different formats, different ethical agreements.
- How to lead a small collaboration without losing the project's coherence.
- How to defend choices under expert critique.
- How to write so that both engineers and clinicians can read it.

## Signal that you are ready for the next stage

You have crossed into PhD-level work when:

- You can defend originality, rigor, depth, contribution, and independence — the five qualities — for your own work.
- Your contribution would be a real loss to the field if it disappeared.
- You can name three follow-up directions that depend on what you have built.
- Senior people in the field treat you as the person to ask about your subarea.
