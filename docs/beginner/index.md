# Beginner level

> At the beginner level, R&D means **learning how to investigate a problem and create something useful from what you learn.**

You are not expected to invent anything new yet. You are expected to learn the field deeply enough that you can ask honest questions and build small, working things from the answers.

## What this section covers

- **[What R&D means at this level](what-it-means.md)** — the single sentence to anchor on.
- **[Doing research as a beginner](research.md)** — reading papers, collecting data, drawing conclusions you can defend.
- **[Doing development as a beginner](development.md)** — designing, coding, testing, documenting small artifacts.
- **[Your first R&D project](first-project.md)** — what a complete beginner R&D project actually looks like, end to end.

## What success looks like at this level

You finish a beginner-level R&D project when you can show someone:

- A short write-up of **what you wanted to learn** and **what you actually found**.
- A small piece of code, notebook, or workflow that demonstrates it.
- A clear list of **what you would do differently next time**.

That third item is what makes it R&D and not just an exercise. You are not just doing the task — you are reflecting on the loop between learning and building.

## What you do not have to be yet

- Original. You are reproducing things others have already done.
- Rigorous to PhD standards. You are building intuition.
- Production-ready. Your code is allowed to be ugly.
- Comprehensive. You are picking one small slice of one problem.

The point of this level is to learn the *shape* of an R&D project. Quality follows later.

## Where to next

When the beginner level feels comfortable — when you can read a paper, replicate a small experiment, and write up what happened — move on to [Intermediate — the process](../intermediate/index.md). That is where you start naming the eight steps you have already been doing informally.
