# Independence

> A PhD-level person can **define and lead** a research direction.

Independence is the slowest of the five qualities to grow. It is also the one that most determines what comes after the PhD — postdoc, faculty, principal scientist, founder, or technical lead. The work at the higher levels of any of those careers requires that you can choose the direction yourself.

## What independence looks like

A PhD-level researcher with independence can say, on a whiteboard, with no preparation, in one tight paragraph each:

### "This is the important problem."

- Who has it.
- Why it matters now.
- Why solving it is non-trivial.
- What "solved" would let people do.

### "This is why it matters."

- Clinical, scientific, or societal impact.
- The downstream consequences of getting it right.
- The cost of not getting it right.

### "This is what has been tried."

- The major existing approaches in two or three sentences each.
- Where each one falls short.
- The papers and groups associated with each.

### "This is what is missing."

- The gap, in one sentence.
- Why nobody has closed it (yet).
- What changed recently that makes closing it possible now.

### "This is my approach."

- The high-level idea, in one or two sentences.
- The key insight that makes it different.
- The dependencies on data, methods, and tools.

### "This is how I will validate it."

- The dataset(s).
- The metrics.
- The baselines.
- The external validation plan.

### "This is the contribution to the field."

- The single most important thing the field will be able to do — or stop doing — after this work.

If you can fluently say all seven, you are operating with independence. If any of the seven gets stuck, that is the part to work on.

## What independence is not

- **Working alone.** Independence is the *ability* to set direction, not the choice to do everything yourself. Some of the most independent senior researchers run large collaborative teams.
- **Disagreeing with your advisor.** Independence is not contrarianism; it is the ability to choose direction *whether or not* it aligns with someone else's preferences.
- **Working without input.** Independent researchers consult constantly. They just synthesise the input themselves rather than waiting for someone to assemble it for them.

## How independence is built

### Pick the next project yourself

The single biggest leap is choosing a project — even a small one — without being told what to do. The choice will probably not be optimal. That is the point: the next time will be better.

### Defend your direction

Independence is built by *publicly justifying choices*. Lab meetings, advisor meetings, conference Q&A. Every time you stand behind a choice and answer hard questions, you grow.

### Build an opinion on the field

Spend an afternoon writing your honest answer to *"what are the three most important open problems in this subfield right now, and why?"* Update the document every six months. Track how your answers change as you read more.

### Develop a research aesthetic

Over time you develop preferences — for problems with clinical impact, for methods that scale, for transparent reporting, for open data. Your aesthetic is your direction. Becoming able to articulate it is part of independence.

### Find a peer cohort

You learn independence in part from peers who are also building it. Find or build a small group of people at similar stages who push each other to defend choices and read deeply. This is the closest thing to a cheat code.

## How independence shows up

Independence is visible when:

- A reviewer asks "why did you choose X over Y?" and you have a real answer.
- You read a new paper and immediately know whether it changes your direction.
- You can mentor a junior student through choosing their own project.
- You can write a credible grant proposal or thesis prospectus end-to-end.
- You can hold the project's direction when collaborators push in conflicting ways.

If a senior researcher in your area can say *"X is doing important work on Y, and they know why,"* you have arrived.

## The PhD as a forcing function

The structure of a PhD — original work, thesis, defence — is designed to force independence. The reason the process is uncomfortable is that the discomfort is the curriculum. You do not learn independence from books. You learn it by being the person who has to decide.

If you are early in a PhD and feel underprepared to choose directions: that is normal. If you are late in a PhD and still feel that way: it is fixable, but you have to deliberately do the things on this page.
