# Contribution

> Your work gives others **something useful.**

Originality, rigor, and depth converge in contribution. If the work does not give somebody else something they can use, build on, or argue with, the contribution is private.

## What "useful to others" means

A PhD-level contribution gives at least one of these to the field:

### A paper that changes understanding

A paper at this level is not just *"here is a new number on dataset X."* It changes how people in the field think about something. It might:

- Resolve a contested question.
- Reframe a problem.
- Identify a confound everyone was ignoring.
- Show that an assumed result is actually fragile.
- Demonstrate a transfer between domains nobody had connected.

The paper is the most common contribution surface; it is not the only one.

### A tool others can use

A piece of software that lets other researchers do something they could not easily do before. Successful tools tend to be:

- Solving a real, recurring problem.
- Easier to install than to reproduce.
- Documented for users, not just authors.
- Maintained for at least the duration of their first big paper cycle.

A tool that twenty other groups use is a larger contribution than a paper twenty groups cite but do not act on.

### A dataset others can evaluate on

A released dataset can be a much bigger contribution than the paper that uses it. Successful dataset releases tend to be:

- Carefully curated and labelled.
- Documented with a datasheet.
- Released with a clear license and access process.
- Designed for a specific evaluation task.
- Validated by use across multiple groups.

A common-task benchmark that becomes standard is one of the highest-impact contributions a PhD can make.

### A method others can build on

A method becomes a contribution when other people use it as a baseline, an ingredient, or a starting point. That requires:

- A clear formulation.
- An open-source reference implementation.
- Reproducible numbers on a public dataset.
- A clean conceptual separation from existing methods.

### A framework that organises the field

Sometimes the contribution is conceptual: a taxonomy, an evaluation protocol, a survey that names categories everyone subsequently uses.

These are rare and high-impact. They require both the depth to see the structure and the writing to communicate it.

### A validated biomarker

In biomedical AI specifically, a biomarker that has been:

- Defined in terms of measurable inputs,
- Linked to a clinical outcome,
- Validated across multiple sites or cohorts, and
- Released as a runnable workflow,

is a major contribution. It is also one of the hardest to achieve, because all of validation, science, and engineering have to come together.

## What does not count as contribution

- **A paper nobody cites except your group.** The work may still be valuable to you; it has not yet contributed to the field.
- **Code on GitHub with no users.** Releasing it is necessary but not sufficient.
- **A dataset behind a barrier so high nobody uses it.** Access matters.
- **A method that improves SOTA by 0.5% on one benchmark.** That is a contribution to that benchmark, not to understanding.

The honest test: *"five years from now, what will the field have differently because of this work?"* If you cannot answer, the contribution is not yet sharp.

## How to maximise contribution

### Release more than the paper

The paper is the lowest-bandwidth artifact. Add:

- The code.
- The trained models or checkpoints.
- The exact data splits.
- The evaluation script.
- The configs that produced the headline numbers.
- A short blog post or thread aimed at non-specialists.

Each of these increases the chance that someone else uses the work.

### Write for the next user, not for the reviewer

The reviewer reads it once and decides. The next user reads it monthly for a year. Document for the user — clear examples, common pitfalls, contact channels.

### Show up

Conferences, workshops, lab visits, informal Q&A. Contributions become known partly through reading and partly through people. Both matter.

### Listen to who picks it up

If a group uses your dataset in a way you did not anticipate, or someone adapts your tool to a problem you did not consider, those are signals about where the contribution actually lands. Track them. They will shape your next project.
