# Depth

> You understand the problem **deeply.**

Depth is the quality that makes everything else possible. Without it, you cannot tell whether your idea is original. You cannot tell whether your evaluation is rigorous. You cannot tell whether your contribution matters. You cannot lead a direction because you do not know what the directions are.

## What deep understanding looks like

A PhD-level researcher in their subfield knows:

### The history of the field

- The major eras and what shifted between them.
- Which papers caused the shifts.
- Which methods are dominant now and which used to be.
- Why the dominant methods became dominant.

You do not need to memorise dates. You need to be able to draw a one-page timeline that explains how the field got here.

### The major papers

- The foundational papers everyone cites.
- The papers your subfield's senior people would say are required reading.
- The papers that disagreed with each other and what the resolution was — or was not.

A useful target: you can list ten papers, explain each in two sentences, and place each on the timeline.

### The existing methods

- What methods exist for your problem and adjacent problems.
- Their inputs, outputs, assumptions, and computational profiles.
- Their known failure modes.
- Their open-source implementations and which are maintained.

If a colleague asks *"what would happen if we tried method X here?"* you can answer with a paragraph, not a shrug.

### Their assumptions

- The technical assumptions baked into each method (linearity, stationarity, Gaussian noise, etc.).
- The data assumptions (sample size, label quality, site homogeneity).
- The implicit clinical assumptions (population, deployment surface, decision context).

Reading methods sections for assumptions is a specific skill. It is what lets you see the gap between *"this method works in this paper"* and *"this method will work in my problem."*

### Their limitations

- Where each method breaks down.
- Why.
- What the workaround is, if any.

Limitations sections of papers are gold for this; running other people's code is silver.

### What remains unsolved

- The open problems in your subfield.
- Which of them are open because they are hard, which because nobody has tried, which because the data does not exist.
- Which would unblock other problems if solved.

This is the question that produces thesis topics. It cannot be answered without the previous five layers of depth.

## How depth is built

### Read deeply, not just widely

A few papers read carefully — including running their code, redoing their analysis on different data, and reading the limitations critically — beats fifty abstracts.

### Talk to senior people

Twenty minutes with someone who has been in the field for a decade is worth a week of reading. They tell you what is settled, what is contested, and what people will not write down in papers.

### Teach the material

You discover the holes in your understanding when you try to teach it. Give a journal-club talk, write a tutorial, mentor a more junior student. The questions you cannot answer are your reading list.

### Reproduce results

The single fastest way to see assumptions in a method is to run its code on data slightly different from what the paper used. The gap between "the paper says X" and "in practice you get Y" is where depth lives.

### Sit in the clinic (or wet lab)

For biomedical AI specifically: spending real time with the clinicians or wet-lab researchers whose problem you are working on is irreplaceable. It rewires your sense of what is feasible, what matters, and what people will trust.

## Depth and time

Depth is built over years, not weeks. There is no shortcut.

The PhD discipline is to **invest in depth even when there is no immediate result**. The reading you do today rarely shows up in a paper this quarter. It shows up two years from now, when you can identify the next big question because you can see the field's blind spot — and you can only see it because you have been looking.

## How depth shows up

Depth is invisible until it is needed. It shows up when:

- A reviewer asks an objection nobody else has thought of, and you have the answer.
- A new paper appears and you can tell within a day whether it actually changes anything.
- A colleague is stuck and you can name the assumption they are missing.
- You can identify, at the start of a project, the three most likely ways it will fail.

A senior researcher's depth is what makes everything they touch look easier than it is.
