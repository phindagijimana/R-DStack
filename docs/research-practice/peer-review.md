# Peer review

> Peer review is how findings become knowledge. It is also how careers are built — both as an author and as a reviewer.

This page covers three directions:

1. **Submitting a paper** to a peer-reviewed venue.
2. **Receiving and responding to reviews** of your own work.
3. **Reviewing other people's work**.

All three are skills. All three matter at PhD level and beyond.

## Submitting a paper

### Choosing a journal or conference

Pick by audience first, prestige second:

- **Who reads it?** Your target audience for the work.
- **What is the typical paper there?** Read three recent papers from the venue. Does your work fit?
- **What is the scope?** Methods venues do not publish purely clinical papers; clinical venues do not publish purely methods papers.
- **What is the review timeline?** Some journals take weeks; some take a year. Plan accordingly.
- **What is the impact factor and acceptance rate?** Useful as a coarse signal, not as the decision driver.

For biomedical AI specifically, conferences (MICCAI, NeurIPS, ICML, ICLR, IPMI) carry more weight than they do in many fields. Plan around their deadlines.

### Pre-submission checklist

- The cover letter: one page, explains what the paper does and why it fits this venue.
- The manuscript matches the journal's word count, figure count, reference limit, and structure.
- A reporting guideline from EQUATOR ([CONSORT](https://www.consort-statement.org), [STROBE](https://www.strobe-statement.org), [TRIPOD+AI](https://www.tripod-statement.org), [PRISMA](https://www.prisma-statement.org), etc.) is followed.
- Suggested reviewers (if asked): three to five names of researchers who could fairly review, without conflicts.
- Excluded reviewers (if relevant): people with serious conflicts.
- Data and code availability statement is honest about what is shared.
- All authors agree on the version being submitted.
- ICMJE authorship criteria are met by every listed author. See [Ethics and IRB](ethics-and-irb.md).
- Conflicts of interest are disclosed.

### The cover letter

The cover letter is a sales document for the editor, not the reviewer. A working template:

```
Dear Editor,

We submit for your consideration our manuscript, "TITLE," which we believe
fits the scope of JOURNAL because [one sentence].

The contribution is [one to two sentences]. The work matters because
[one sentence].

The methods are [one sentence on what was done]. The headline result is
[one sentence with a number].

The work has not been published elsewhere and is not under review elsewhere.
All authors have approved the submission.

Sincerely,
[Corresponding author]
```

That is one page. Short, specific, no overclaiming.

## Receiving reviews

Reviews can land as: **accept** (rare), **minor revision**, **major revision**, **reject and resubmit**, or **reject**. None are personal.

When the reviews arrive:

- **Read them once.** Do not respond yet.
- **Walk away for a day.** Reviews provoke reactions; the reaction is rarely useful.
- **Sort the comments**: substantive (need real work), wording / clarity (easy), misunderstandings (need clearer writing).

Then plan the response.

## Responding to reviews

A response letter has two audiences: **the editor**, who is deciding whether to send it back to the reviewers, and **the reviewers**, who are deciding whether their concerns are addressed.

The structure that works:

```
We thank the Editor and Reviewers for their careful reading. Below we
respond to each comment point by point. Reviewer comments are in italics;
our responses are in standard type. Changes to the manuscript are quoted
in blockquote with page references.

Reviewer 1

R1.1: [pasted reviewer comment]

We agree with the reviewer that ... We have [done X], and have added the
following text to page Y line Z:

> [exact text added]

R1.2: ...
```

A few rules:

- **Address every comment**, even minor ones. Skipping signals dismissiveness.
- **Be polite, even when the reviewer is wrong.** Especially when the reviewer is wrong.
- **When you disagree, explain — do not deflect.** "We respectfully disagree because ..." with a substantive reason is far more powerful than no response.
- **Quote the changes inline.** The reviewer should not have to hunt for them.
- **Color-code in the manuscript** if the journal allows: blue text = added, struck text = removed.

A reviewer who is heard is much more likely to recommend acceptance, even if you did not do every suggested change.

## Reviewing other people's papers

Doing reviews is part of the job. It is also one of the best ways to learn what good and bad papers look like, fast.

### What an editor wants from a review

- A **summary** showing you understood the paper.
- A **recommendation** (accept / minor / major / reject) supported by reasons.
- **Major concerns** with the science: design, statistics, baselines, generalisation, conclusions.
- **Minor concerns** with clarity and presentation.
- **Tone that is professional**.

What an editor does *not* want:

- A list of every typo (note "there are typos throughout"; do not enumerate).
- Personal attacks.
- Demands that the authors run a different study (they wrote this one).
- Two-paragraph reviews that say nothing the editor cannot already see.

### A review template

```
Summary
- Two to three sentences in your own words.

Major comments
- 1. The largest scientific or methodological concern.
- 2. The next largest.
- 3. ...

Minor comments
- 4. Clarity, presentation, citations.

Recommendation
- (Optional, depending on journal policy.)
```

Aim for 600–1500 words. A 200-word review undersells your work; a 5000-word review buries the editor.

### Time budget

A solid review takes three to six hours. Block the time on your calendar before you accept the assignment.

### Confidentiality

Reviewing material is confidential — do not share, post, or feed to AI tools without checking the journal's policy. **[COPE](https://publicationethics.org)** has detailed guidance on reviewer ethics.

## When to decline a review

Decline if:

- You have a conflict of interest with the authors.
- You do not have time within the deadline.
- The paper is outside your expertise.

Declining responsibly — with a few suggestions of alternative reviewers — is more professional than accepting and producing a poor review.

## Beyond traditional peer review

Some communities are moving toward newer models:

- **Open peer review** — reviews and identities are published alongside the paper.
- **Post-publication peer review** — venues like PubPeer let readers comment after publication.
- **Continuous review** — preprint servers + community comments.
- **Registered reports** — protocols are reviewed *before* the study; acceptance is decided on the protocol's quality.

Each has tradeoffs. Knowing they exist matters more than picking a "best" one.
