# Writing fundamentals

> The first job of a sentence is to be understood. Almost everything else follows from that.

Most researchers were never taught to write. That is not their fault — most graduate programs assume writing is something you picked up. The result is a body of literature that is harder to read than it has to be. The good news: a small number of concrete rules eliminate most of the friction.

This page collects the rules and the institutional resources that already do this work well, including the two you flagged.

## The Hamilton "Seven Rules of Writing"

The Hamilton College Writing Center maintains a list of the seven mechanical issues that cause most of the trouble in academic prose. The whole list is on their site — read it once, refer back to it forever:

> 📚 **[Hamilton College — Seven Rules of Writing](https://www.hamilton.edu/academics/centers/writing/seven-sins-of-writing)**

The seven, in summary:

1. **Passive voice.** Prefer the active voice — *"we computed the asymmetry index"* beats *"the asymmetry index was computed."* Use passive only when the actor is genuinely unknown or unimportant.
2. **Punctuation of two independent clauses.** Two complete sentences joined with a comma is a comma splice; use a period, a semicolon, or a conjunction.
3. **Concise writing.** Cut every word that does not change the meaning. "Due to the fact that" → "because."
4. **Using the apostrophe.** Apostrophes show possession, not plurality. *Patients'* (belonging to patients), not *patient's* (one patient's), not *patients* (more than one).
5. **Using modifiers.** Place modifiers next to the things they modify. *"Only the model achieved 0.84 AUC"* is different from *"the model only achieved 0.84 AUC"* is different from *"the model achieved only 0.84 AUC."*
6. **Using pronouns.** Make sure every pronoun has an unambiguous antecedent. If a reader has to re-read a sentence to know what *"it"* refers to, the sentence is wrong.
7. **Commonly confused words.** *Affect / effect.* *Less / fewer.* *Comprise / compose.* *Principal / principle.* Look them up; the field notices.

Apply the seven before you submit anything. They take an hour to internalise and then save you embarrassment for the rest of your career.

## The Northwestern CLIMB principles

Northwestern University's CLIMB (Career Learning in the Mathematical and Biological sciences) program publishes some of the best institutional guidance on scientific writing on the open web. The full hub:

> 📚 **[Northwestern CLIMB — Written Communication](https://www.northwestern.edu/climb/resources/written-communication/)**

Two of their pieces are particularly worth internalising:

### The five principles for readable sentences

CLIMB's distillation of decades of usability research on scientific prose. A high-level version:

- **Subject and verb close together** — readers parse the action right away.
- **Old information before new** — each sentence opens with what the reader already knows and ends with what is new. Stories flow.
- **Strong verbs do the work** — verbs carry meaning; do not nominalise them into nouns (*"we observed"* beats *"observations were made"*).
- **One main idea per sentence** — when in doubt, split.
- **Concrete subjects** — abstract subjects (*"the analysis"*) drift; concrete ones (*"we"*, *"the model"*) land.

### Coherent paragraphs

CLIMB's recommendation, distilled from rhetoric and composition research:

- **A topic sentence opens the paragraph.** It tells the reader what the paragraph will argue.
- **Echo words tie the paragraph together.** Repeat a key noun or phrase across sentences instead of substituting synonyms.
- **Transitions make the logical move visible.** *However*, *therefore*, *for example*, *by contrast*.
- **A closing sentence delivers the point.** State the consequence of the paragraph, not just the last detail.

A paragraph without a topic sentence, echo words, and a closing point is a paragraph the reader is forced to summarise themselves. They will not.

## Schimel — Stories beat lists

Joshua Schimel's *Writing Science* (Oxford University Press, 2012) is the most-recommended book on scientific writing among working scientists, for one reason:

> Schimel argues that **scientific papers are stories**, not lists of facts. Facts in a list are forgotten; facts inside a narrative are remembered.

The narrative arc for a paper:

- **Opening** — set the scene. What was known? Why does it matter?
- **Challenge** — name the unsolved problem.
- **Action** — what did you do?
- **Resolution** — what did you find?
- **Closing** — what does it now mean?

This structure is also the structure of a good talk, a good grant, and a good README. Once you see it, you cannot unsee it.

## Pinker — Modern clarity

Steven Pinker's *The Sense of Style* (Penguin, 2014) is the contemporary heir to *The Elements of Style*. The most useful chapters for researchers:

- **The Curse of Knowledge** — why experts write incomprehensibly: they forget what a non-expert does not know. Antidotes: write for a reader exactly one notch less expert than you are; read your draft aloud; have a non-expert read a paragraph and tell you what they think it says.
- **Coherence** — the structure of an argument. Pinker formalises what CLIMB summarises.
- **Right and wrong** — a calmer, evidence-based take on grammar than the prescriptivist tradition. Useful when reviewers cite "rules" that are not rules.

## A pre-submission checklist

Before any draft goes anywhere — co-author, advisor, journal — run it through:

- [ ] Every paragraph has a topic sentence.
- [ ] No comma splices.
- [ ] No passive sentences without a reason.
- [ ] No more than 25 words per sentence on average (look at your longest five).
- [ ] No "in order to," no "due to the fact that," no "it has been demonstrated that."
- [ ] Every "this" or "it" has an unambiguous antecedent.
- [ ] Every figure has a caption that stands alone.
- [ ] Numbers in the abstract match numbers in the results.
- [ ] Citations are formatted, complete, and live (no `[?]`).
- [ ] You read the whole thing aloud once.

## Reading aloud

The single highest-yield writing habit. The eye accepts what the ear rejects. If you stumble reading a sentence aloud, the reader will stumble silently. Re-write it.
