# Preprints and publishing

> Where you put the paper shapes who reads it, when they read it, and what they can do with it.

## Preprints

A preprint is the manuscript posted publicly *before* peer review. The major servers:

- **[arXiv](https://arxiv.org)** — the original. Dominant in physics, math, CS. Used by AI/ML.
- **[bioRxiv](https://www.biorxiv.org)** — biology.
- **[medRxiv](https://www.medrxiv.org)** — clinical and epidemiology.
- **[Research Square](https://www.researchsquare.com)** — multidisciplinary.
- **[ChemRxiv](https://chemrxiv.org)**, **[PsyArXiv](https://psyarxiv.com)**, **[OSF Preprints](https://osf.io/preprints/)** — disciplinary servers.

### Why preprint

- **Speed.** You stake a flag in the timeline immediately. Peer review can take six months to two years; the work exists in the world meanwhile.
- **Feedback.** Readers comment publicly or by email. Some of this feedback improves the paper before submission.
- **Discoverability.** Preprints are indexed by Google Scholar and increasingly by PubMed. Citations accrue.
- **Open by default.** Anyone can read it, regardless of institutional access.

### Concerns

- Some journals (a shrinking list) consider a preprint to be "prior publication" and decline submission. Check [Sherpa Romeo](https://v2.sherpa.ac.uk/romeo/) or the journal's policy first.
- Once posted, the manuscript cannot be retracted — only revised. Make sure the version is one you would defend.
- Some clinical journals require an embargo for press-attention reasons; check.

For biomedical AI in 2025+, posting to arXiv (methods) or bioRxiv / medRxiv (clinical) before journal submission is the default for most groups.

### Versioning preprints

Each revised version of the paper can be posted as a new version on the same DOI. Track:

- v1 — initial submission.
- v2 — after major revision.
- v3 — final accepted version (the "post-print" or AAM).

Both reviewers and readers can compare versions. This is also a useful feature when you eventually defend a thesis.

## Choosing a journal

A working framework:

### 1. Scope fit

Read the journal's *Aims and Scope* page and three recent papers. Does your paper look like those? If not, do not submit.

### 2. Audience

The journal is a distribution channel. Who do you want to read this work — methodologists, clinicians, the broad scientific public, the regulatory community? Pick accordingly.

### 3. Impact factor (used carefully)

Impact factor is a *coarse* signal of reach. It is also widely abused. Use it as a tie-breaker between two journals you would otherwise pick. Do not use it as the only criterion.

The San Francisco Declaration on Research Assessment (DORA) and the Leiden Manifesto both warn against impact-factor-driven assessment of *individuals* and *papers*. Many funders now formally agree.

### 4. Review timeline

Some journals decide in eight weeks; some in eight months. If you are on a thesis or job-market deadline, this matters.

### 5. Open access policies

Increasingly important. Three flavours:

- **Gold open access** — the journal makes the paper free to read; authors typically pay an article processing charge (APC), often $1,500–$11,000.
- **Green open access** — the author posts the accepted manuscript in a repository (preprint server, institutional repo) after an embargo.
- **Diamond / platinum open access** — no APC, no paywall. Funded by societies or institutions.

Your funder almost certainly has a policy. NIH, Wellcome, ERC, and most others now require some form of open access.

- **[DOAJ — Directory of Open Access Journals](https://doaj.org)** — vetted open-access journals.
- **[Sherpa Romeo](https://v2.sherpa.ac.uk/romeo/)** — open-access and self-archiving policies by journal.
- **[Plan S](https://www.coalition-s.org)** — the major funder coalition's open-access policy.

### 6. Reputation and integrity

Avoid:

- Predatory journals (see [Searching the literature → Predatory outlets](literature-search.md#predatory-and-low-quality-outlets)).
- Journals with high rates of retraction or COPE complaints.
- Pay-to-publish journals that promise review in three days.

Check by venue:

- Indexed in PubMed / Web of Science.
- Listed in DOAJ if open access.
- Editorial board includes people you recognise as legitimate.

## After acceptance

Once a paper is accepted:

- **Page proofs.** Read them carefully. Most last-minute errors slip in here.
- **Permissions.** If you reuse figures from anywhere — including your own previously published work — you need permission.
- **Embargo.** Some journals embargo press coverage and pre-publication discussion.
- **DOI.** The paper gets a permanent identifier; update your CV, ORCID, Google Scholar, lab website.
- **Self-archiving.** If your funder requires it, upload the accepted manuscript to a repository.

## After publication

- **Promote it.** A short thread, an internal seminar, a slide for your next talk. Even one minute of effort dramatically increases who reads it.
- **Update your repos.** Add the citation to the GitHub README. Tag the code release with the journal DOI.
- **Track citations and engagement.** Scholar, Web of Science, Altmetric.
- **Respond to questions and bug reports.** Your paper now has users.

## When a paper is rejected

Reject decisions sting. They also happen to everyone, repeatedly.

- **Read the reviews honestly.** A surprising fraction of rejection reviews contain good advice.
- **Decide quickly.** Will you appeal, transfer to a sister journal, or submit somewhere else?
- **Revise based on the reviews.** Even if you cannot use this round, the next reviewers will see a stronger paper.
- **Track the cycle.** Most papers take two or three submission attempts before acceptance.

The work itself does not change because a journal rejected it. Only its label changes.
