# Grant writing

> Grants are the writing artifact that funds the next direction. PhD-level R&D depends on grant writing whether or not you stay in academia.

This page focuses on the U.S. NIH and NSF systems — both because they are dominant for biomedical AI and because their resources are the most freely available. The principles transfer to other systems.

## Why grant writing is its own craft

A paper persuades a reviewer that *you did the thing*. A grant persuades a reviewer that *you will do the thing* and that *the thing matters*. Different audience, different tense, different rhetorical strategy.

The CLIMB program at Northwestern publishes the most useful institutional guide to grant proposal structure on the open web:

> 📚 **[Northwestern CLIMB — Written Communication](https://www.northwestern.edu/climb/resources/written-communication/)**

Specifically their Aims Page architecture, NIH review criteria, and Significance / Innovation / Approach materials. Read them. The rest of this page references and complements them.

## The funding landscape

### NIH mechanisms (selected)

Career and training:

- **F30 / F31** — predoctoral fellowships (MD/PhD, PhD).
- **F32** — postdoctoral fellowships.
- **K99 / R00** — "Pathway to Independence." Postdoc-to-faculty bridge.
- **K01 / K08 / K23 / K25** — mentored career development awards for various career stages.

Research:

- **R03** — small grants, two years.
- **R21** — exploratory / developmental, two years, higher risk.
- **R01** — the standard investigator-initiated research project, three to five years.
- **U01 / U24** — cooperative agreements, often consortium-scale.
- **P-series** — program project / center grants.

### NSF mechanisms (selected)

- **GRFP** — Graduate Research Fellowship.
- **Standard, CAREER, CRII, MRI** — research and infrastructure.
- **SBIR / STTR** — small business / tech transfer.

Other funders:

- **HHMI, Wellcome, Gates, Helmsley, Chan Zuckerberg** — large private foundations.
- **DoD CDMRP, NIH BRAIN Initiative** — targeted national programs.

Each mechanism has its own page count, review criteria, and budget rules. Read the call before drafting.

### The official sources

These are the authoritative documents. They are also dense; CLIMB and your institution's grants office translate them.

- **[NIH Grants](https://grants.nih.gov)** — funding announcements, application instructions, review criteria.
- **[NIH NIAID Sample Applications](https://www.niaid.nih.gov/grants-contracts/sample-applications)** — actual funded applications, with reviewer summary statements. Read at least three before writing your first one.
- **[NSF PAPPG — Proposal and Award Policies and Procedures Guide](https://www.nsf.gov/pubs/policydocs/pappg)** — the NSF operating manual.
- **[NIH Review Criteria](https://grants.nih.gov/grants/peer-review.htm)** — the rubrics reviewers actually use.

## The Specific Aims page

For NIH grants, the **Specific Aims page is the single most important page in your application.** Many reviewers read only the Aims page closely; everything else is consulted to confirm or refute their impression of it.

Northwestern CLIMB calls out a four-part architecture. Distilled:

### Paragraph 1 — Importance

- Establish the broad area's importance in 2–3 sentences.
- Use clinical or biological stakes that a non-specialist reviewer would care about.

### Paragraph 2 — Gap

- What is currently known.
- What is currently unknown.
- Why closing the gap matters.

### Paragraph 3 — Long-term goal, central hypothesis, objective

- **Long-term goal** — your career-scale aim.
- **Central hypothesis** — the testable statement this project addresses.
- **Objective of this proposal** — the specific question you will answer in this funding cycle.
- **Rationale** — why this project, why now, why you.

### Paragraph 4 — Specific Aims

Two to three aims, each as a one-sentence bullet:

- **Aim 1.** [Action verb] [what you will do]. *Hypothesis.* *Approach summary.*
- **Aim 2.** ...
- **Aim 3.** ...

Aims are **independent enough** that failure of one does not collapse the others.

### Paragraph 5 — Innovation and impact

- What is novel about the proposal.
- What the field gains if the project succeeds.
- Connection back to the long-term goal.

A well-constructed Aims page fits on one page in 11-point font. It does the entire job of selling the project.

## NIH review criteria

NIH reviewers score on five criteria (recently restructured to three factors, but the underlying questions persist):

1. **Significance** — does the project matter?
2. **Innovation** — is the approach novel?
3. **Approach** — is the design rigorous?
4. **Investigator** — can the team do it?
5. **Environment** — does the institution support it?

Each section of your proposal should map directly onto these. Reviewers fill in scoring sheets; you make their job easier by making the mapping obvious.

## The full NIH research strategy (12 pages for R01)

After the Aims page comes the research strategy. Sub-sections:

### Significance (~2 pages)

- The scientific and clinical importance of the problem.
- Current state of the field and its limitations.
- How this project will advance the field.

The CLIMB Significance materials are excellent here.

### Innovation (~1 page)

- What is conceptually, methodologically, or technically novel.
- How it differs from existing approaches.

### Approach (~7–9 pages)

For each Specific Aim:

- **Rationale and hypothesis.**
- **Preliminary data** showing feasibility.
- **Experimental design** with controls.
- **Statistical analysis plan** with power.
- **Expected outcomes** that map back to the hypothesis.
- **Pitfalls and alternative strategies** — name what could go wrong and what you would do instead.

Reviewers reward authors who have thought through failure modes. They penalise authors who pretend nothing can go wrong.

## Writing for non-specialists

A study section is composed of researchers in adjacent fields, not your sub-sub-field. Write so that an immunologist can review your imaging-genomics proposal:

- Define jargon on first use, or avoid it.
- Use figures to convey concepts.
- State implications in clinical or biological terms.
- Avoid acronyms that have not been spelled out.

The Curse of Knowledge ([Pinker, *The Sense of Style*](writing-fundamentals.md#pinker-modern-clarity)) is the biggest enemy of grant writing.

## Budget basics

- **Personnel costs** dominate most grants. Be honest about percent effort.
- **Equipment** above a threshold (often $5,000) is itemised.
- **Travel** and **publication costs** are often allowed.
- **Indirect costs** (overhead) are negotiated between your institution and the funder.

Your institution's grants office reviews budgets before submission. Use them — that is what they are for.

## Common pitfalls

- **Over-promising and under-detailing.** Reviewers see this immediately.
- **No alternative strategies.** A grant without "what if this doesn't work" feels naive.
- **Too many aims.** Three is the working maximum.
- **Aims that depend on each other.** Aim 1 fails → Aim 2 collapses → Aim 3 was never going to work.
- **Boilerplate Significance.** Reviewers smell copy-paste from other proposals.
- **No preliminary data.** For most mechanisms, you need at least pilot data showing feasibility.
- **Budget mismatch.** A budget that does not match the proposed work signals incompetence.

## A timeline for writing an R01

A first R01 typically takes four to six months of part-time writing:

- **Month 1** — Specific Aims iteration with mentor / colleagues.
- **Month 2** — Significance and Innovation; preliminary data figures.
- **Months 3–4** — Approach. The longest section.
- **Month 5** — Other components (biosketches, budget, letters of support, facilities).
- **Month 6** — Polish, internal review, submission.

Submit earlier than the deadline. NIH and NSF submission systems are unreliable in the final 24 hours.

## Resubmission

Most grants are not funded on the first submission. NIH allows one resubmission with a clear "Introduction to the Resubmission" page addressing prior reviewer concerns. Treat the resubmission like a paper response letter — be thorough, polite, and substantive.
